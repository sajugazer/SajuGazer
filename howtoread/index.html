How to Read Saju

GuideBasics

The Map of Destiny

Manseoryeok (the Ten-Thousand Year Calendar) is often called the "map of destiny." In the modern era, you no longer need to perform complex astronomical calculations yourself; simply entering your birth date and time into a smartphone app or website instantly reveals eight Chinese characters. Learning to interpret these characters is the very first step in Saju (Four Pillars) analysis.

However, merely generating a chart is only the beginning. The real skill lies in understanding the relationships between these characters—how they support, restrain, combine, and transform one another. Think of it as reading a musical score: the individual notes matter, but it is the harmony and tension between them that create the melody of your life. This guide will walk you through the essential building blocks, step by step.

The Four Pillars

When you open a Manseoryeok, the first things you’ll notice are the four columns arranged from right to left. Starting from the right, these are called the Year Pillar (Yeon-ju), Month Pillar (Wol-ju), Day Pillar (Il-ju), and Hour Pillar (Si-ju). Each pillar consists of two characters, one above the other. The top character is known as the Heavenly Stem (Cheongan), while the bottom is the Earthly Branch (Jiji). The core of the analysis lies in examining the complex interactions between these eight characters.

Each pillar also carries a distinct symbolic meaning in terms of life domains. The Year Pillar reflects your social environment, ancestry, and early childhood. The Month Pillar represents your career, parents, and the conditions of your upbringing. The Day Pillar—particularly the Heavenly Stem—embodies your core self and intimate relationships. The Hour Pillar speaks to your later years, children, and innermost ambitions. By reading across all four pillars, a practitioner can construct a holistic portrait of someone's life trajectory.

The Day Master

The most important protagonist in a Saju structure is the Ilgan (Daily Stem), which is the top character of the Day Pillar. The Ilgan symbolizes your true essence and the core of your "self." For instance, if your Ilgan is 'Gap' (甲), you are seen as having a temperament like a towering tree—possessing strong drive and a desire for honor. On the other hand, if it is 'Gye' (癸), you are interpreted as having a flexible and wise nature, much like a gentle rain or a flowing stream.

There are ten possible Ilgan values, each mapped to one of the Five Elements in either its Yin or Yang form. Understanding your Ilgan is akin to knowing your fundamental operating system—it shapes how you process the world, respond to challenges, and relate to others. A strong Ilgan suggests self-reliance, while a weak Ilgan may indicate a person who thrives through collaboration and external support. Neither is inherently better; the key is understanding the balance.

The Month Branch & Five Elements

Once you have identified the Ilgan, the next step is to examine the Wol-ji (Monthly Branch), which represents the season of your birth. The Wol-ji determines the "temperature" and "humidity" of the environment surrounding the Ilgan. For example, a tree born in the height of summer requires completely different elements to survive than a tree born in the middle of winter. The subsequent stage of analysis involves checking the quantity and harmony of the Five Elements (Ohaeng). You look to see which energies—Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, or Water—are excessively dominant or lacking within your Saju.

This process is often called "Ohaeng Bunseok" (Five Element Analysis). A well-balanced chart, where all five elements are present in moderate proportions, tends to indicate a smooth and adaptable life. However, perfect balance is exceedingly rare. Most charts show a distinctive tilt—perhaps an abundance of Fire and a scarcity of Water, or a dominance of Metal with little Wood. These imbalances are not flaws; they are the defining features of your personality and fate, pointing toward both your greatest strengths and the areas where conscious effort is needed.

Yongshin & Decade Luck

If there is too much Fire (火), you need Water (水) to cool the heat or Earth (土) to drain the excess energy. Conversely, if the chart is too cold, the energy of Fire becomes desperate. The decisive character that balances these skewed energies is called the Yongsin (Useful God), and finding it is considered the highlight of a Saju reading. Finally, one must look at the Daewun (Great Fortune). If the Saju-palja (the eight characters) represents the performance of the car you were born with, the Daewun represents the condition of the road that car is driving on. By observing this flow of luck, which shifts in 10-year cycles, you can gauge whether you are currently traveling on a bumpy unpaved road or a wide-open highway.

Beyond Daewun, there are also Sewun (Annual Fortune) and Wolwun (Monthly Fortune), which provide finer-grained timing. Think of Daewun as the climate of a decade, Sewun as the weather of a particular year, and Wolwun as today's forecast. Skilled practitioners layer these cycles together to pinpoint periods of opportunity or caution. This temporal dimension is what makes Saju uniquely actionable—it doesn't just describe who you are, but when certain energies in your life will be amplified or diminished.

The Purpose of Reading Saju

Ultimately, examining one's Saju is not merely about fortune-telling or predicting good and bad luck; it is the process of identifying the distribution of your inherent energies and adjusting the pace of your life accordingly. However, one must avoid becoming overly obsessed. It is best to treat Saju as a tool for gaining insight rather than an absolute truth. Using these findings as a practical guide for self-reflection allows you to maintain a healthy perspective without letting destiny dictate your every move.

As you begin to study Saju, remember that mastery comes gradually. Start by familiarizing yourself with your own Ilgan and the basic Five Element interactions. Over time, you will develop an intuitive sense for how the characters speak to one another. The goal is not to memorize rigid formulas, but to cultivate the kind of pattern recognition that allows you to read a chart the way a seasoned musician reads a complex score—fluidly, holistically, and with appreciation for both the harmonies and the dissonances.
